I saw mitered dovetails featured by [Matt Cremona](https://youtu.be/Ws4OqPIIFms?t=107) and thought they would look good for the carcase of my sideboard. Since the shop had not received more walnut I decided to practice cutting mitered dovetails. I used [Christopher Schwarz's video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CKyzaJeg8r8) as reference for laying out and cutting the joint.

I found two scraps of pine that were priced at $1. They were originally 3/4", but after joining and planing, I was left with 1/2" -- more than enough to practice.

I set dividers to give me fairly wide pins for my first practice board. Besides the usual marks for dovetails, you mark the last two tails on the face and also mark the miters on both edges.

However, I had overcut the pin-board with the coping saw leaving an unsightly gap (light came through the joint). To fix this Michael recommended I cut some glue-blocks out of hardwood (in this case cherry scraps) that would focus the clamping pressure directly onto the tails, helping seat them. The glue-block was made by roughly marking where the tails were and the cutting the surrounding material out with a bandsaw.
<figure>
  <img src ="/img/2019-01-13-mitered-dovetail/glue-block-small.jpg" alt="glue block" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>

I considered doing the same for the pins, however the gap didn't actually go all the way through (chip out from using radial arm saw rather than table saw with cross-cut sled) meaning that glue-blocks wouldn't actually help much. Plus, clamping would have been awkward.

After letting the glue dry, I did cursory cleaning with a scraper.
<figure>
  <img src ="/img/2019-01-13-mitered-dovetail/rough-clean-small.jpg" alt="rough clean" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>

I then hand planed the proud tails and pins, and finished the joint with 120 grit sandpaper by hand.

I don't care much for the angle I used for the dovetails (1/6) -- they look too much like the machine made dovetails. I looked into different angles and decided to try 1/3 for the next practice set.

**Next time**
* Use table saw with cross-cut sled to get clean edges on boards. Otherwise, really make sure that the inside face of the board is face down on radial arm saw.
* Err on the side of too little sawing with coping saw.
* Be careful when cutting miter to not go to far at top; the miter pin gets thinner at top.
* Use steeper angle for dovetails; 1/3 or 1/4.
