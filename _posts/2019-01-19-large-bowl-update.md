The bowls I threw [last Thursday]({{ site.baseurl }}{% post_url 2019-01-13-throwing-large-bowls %}) had finished drying to leather-hard by this Thursday (01-17). I popped them back on the wheel and trimmed them and added feet. I decided to add some more carving/detail this time around and overall, I'm fairly happy with the outcome. Unfortunately, after trimming one of them developed a crack while drying in the bottom.

The first bowl I added a spiral to the bottom of which I was quite pleased with; otherwise, I left the bowl fairly untouched other than a fairly simple foot.
<figure>
  <img src="/img/2019-01-19-large-bowl-update/bowl1-bottom.JPG" alt="bowl1" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>
This was the bowl that after drying overnight developed a crack. This is likely because the bottom was still fairly thick and it had a hard time drying evenly. Even if it surved firing and glazing, this crack would weaken the bowl considerably, making it much more likely to break while in use. So, rather than potentially waste that work I was able to crush the bowl and throw it in a bucket of water. I'll take that out and salvage the clay next week. Perhaps I'll add in some of my other clay and retry another bowl this size.
<figure>
  <img src="/img/2019-01-19-large-bowl-update/bowl1-crack.JPG" alt="crack" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>

For the second bowl I carved a foot into the bottom - and played with a bulls-eye patter (I like the spiral of bowl 1 a lot more) - but left the foot continuous with the outside surface. I then added a band of carving around the bottom inch or two while leaving plenty of space at the very bottom to leave plain for the glaze firing. After carving the band I decided to add three slightly curved triangles going up to the cove near the rim of the bowl. I thought it would make it look more like the carving was an exposed layer underneath the smooth surface. The jury's still out.
<figure>
  <img src="/img/2019-01-19-large-bowl-update/bowl2-bottom.JPG" alt="bowl 2 bottom" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>
<figure>
  <img src="/img/2019-01-19-large-bowl-update/bowl2-side.JPG" alt="bowl 2 side" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>
I think I should have actually carved a slight indent first and then added the carving to give it that extra dimension - which is exactly what I tried in the third bowl!

The third bowl had a more pronounced foot like the first bowl, but the bottom was curved to match the curve of the inside of the bowl. I then carved out a band running around the outside of the bowl and then filled that band with the same kind of carving from the second bowl.
<figure>
  <img src="/img/2019-01-19-large-bowl-update/bowl3-side.JPG" alt="bowl 3" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>
I'm a little happier with the way this turned out dimensionally, although I think I need a cleaner edge between the carving and the smooth surface. I probably should have first outlined the area lightly with the pen tool and then carved it out. This would have also given me a second chance to change the wrapping of the band and make it a little more even. Next time.

I also perused the glazes a little bit and came across a combination that I really want to try. It was labeled "Blue haze reduction" so I need to ask either Jenny or Sarah what exactly that means. I really like the runniness of the blue glaze and think it could look killer on the inside of the bowls. I'm not sure how it would look in the carving itself though.
<figure>
  <img src="/img/2019-01-19-large-bowl-update/blue-haze-reduction.JPG" alt="blue haze reduction" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>
I'm also tempted to do the magnesium/blue haze combination I used before on mom's mug. A last idea would be to mask the carving so that it is actually a different glaze entirely. I'll have to keep thinking.
