The serving bowls I had [thrown]({{ site.baseurl }}{% post_url 2019-01-13-throwing-large-bowls %}) and [trimmed]({{ site.baseurl}}{% post_url 2019-01-19-large-bowl-update %}) previously just came out of the kiln after glazing. I ended up going with the blue haze under Mg<sup>2+</sup> white for the larger bowl and a two tone on the smaller bowl with blue haze in the textured section and 2A23 gray blue haze on the upper section.

As I had done with mom's mug, I only dipped the top portion (in this case ~2-3 inches) in blue haze and then dipped the bowl in Mg<sup>2+</sup> white. I had to pour the Mg<sup>2+</sup> white glaze into a larger tub to allow me to fully submerge the bowl. Even still I had to hold onto the bottom foot and rotate the bowl to get all sides covered. Flipping the bowl over fairly quickly also got me a couple of drips which I was happy with.

Once fired, this glaze turned out pretty well. I like how the Mg<sup>2+</sup> interacted with the texture and I got pretty good blue haze reveal from underneath. I dipped for a little bit longer than the first time I did this combination, so I'll want to ask how amount of blue haze will likely interact with the Mg<sup>2+</sup>. I also really like how Mg<sup>2+</sup> looks on its own, but I'm curious to see how it would interact with some of the other glazes.

Interestingly where the drips formed over blue haze turned a very subtle light purplish-pink which Jenny thought was likely due to tin. However, where it dripped back over the Mg<sup>2+</sup> it just became a clearer white.
<figure>
  <img src="/img/2019-02-11-serving-bowls/bowl2-drips.JPG" alt="" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>
And the textured area that had both glazes completely filled in which is good to know for future projects. If I want to use double glaze on textured areas, then I need to do very light glazing.
<figure>
  <img src="/img/2019-02-11-serving-bowls/bowl2-texture-zoom.JPG" alt="" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>

For the smaller bowl I first painted blue haze into the textured portion of the bowl.
<figure>
  <img src="/img/2019-02-11-serving-bowls/bowl1-blue-haze.JPG" alt="" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>
I then cleaned up the edges and wiped down some of the glaze so that the texture wouldn't be completely filled. I then put a layer of wax on top of the glaze so that I could dip the whole bowl in the second glaze without it sticking to that area. I let this sit for a couple of days to make sure the wax was completely dry.
<figure>
  <img src="/img/2019-02-11-serving-bowls/bowl1-blue-haze-wax.JPG" alt="" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>
I decided to us 2A23 gray blue glaze since the blue haze was likely going to have a little bit of gray in it which I thought would look nice with 2A23 gray blue. Since the blue haze was covered in wax, after I dipped into 2A23 gray blue I was able to wipe off the glaze from the waxed portion.
<figure>
  <img src="/img/2019-02-11-serving-bowls/bowl1-blue-grey.JPG" alt="" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>
The 2A23 gray blue glaze ended up with some really nice gray highlights and a lot more brown than I would have predicted. However, this ended up working well since the thin blue haze on the raised portion of the texture ended up brown.
<figure>
  <img src="/img/2019-02-11-serving-bowls/bowl1-fired.JPG" alt="" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>
<figure>
  <img src="/img/2019-02-11-serving-bowls/bowl1-fired3.JPG" alt="" height="50%" width="50%"/>
</figure>
The texture turned out pretty well, but it is good to now know that if I want the full color of the glaze in the texture I need to make sure that the carving is deep enough. On the left side of this I think there was a combination of not enough glaze getting into the texture as well as too much of it getting wiped off when I applied the wax. I also think that I should have carved a light gouge to better differentiate the two glazes.
